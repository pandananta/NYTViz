We analyzed 3,000+ New York Times titles to see how they evolved over time. This is what we found.

The number of words is increasing.

We measured the average number of words in a title for 10 articles every month over a span of 27 years. The graph on the right shows fluctuations from year-to-year, with a clear upward trend.

Does this surprise you? You are not alone. A whopping 48% or the young adults we surveyed thought that titles were actually getting shorter over time.

But the number of syllables is consistent.

Even though the number of words is increasing, the number of syllables has stayed relatively consistent over time. This suggests that we are using shorter words, but more of them.

Difficulty of title comprehension is decreasing.

We measured the Gunning Fog Index (GFI) of our titles sample for every year. Gunning Fog is a measure readability, estimating how many years of American education you would need in order to comprehend a passage of text. As you can see below, you probably need to complete 8th grade before you could understand a title in 1984. But titles in 2006 were accesisible to 5th graders.

And the density of meaningful words is decreasing.

Finally, we measured the lexical density of titles by year and found it was also decreasing. Lexical density is a measure of content per functional and lexical unit. A low lexical density may signify a high ratio of fluff or stop words.

This will not come as a surprise to most. 78% of respondants thought that news articles were becoming easier to read and less complicated over time.



Titles are the same size as always. But the language is getting simpler.
